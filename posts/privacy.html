Privacy as a Fundamental Right

Published on July 2, 2024 ‚Ä¢ 6 min read

Privacy is not about hiding something because you have something to hide. It's about the right to not have everything out in the open even though I might not have anything to hide. It's about the right to choose what one is willing to disclose to the public.

  • Privacy is a right that needs not be preceded by circumstances where you need to hide something
  • Just as free speech doesn't require that you have something important to say
  • There's no contradiction between self-expression and maintaining privacy
"Arguing that you don't care about the right to privacy because you have nothing to hide is no different than saying you don't care about free speech because you have nothing to say."

The Free Speech Analogy

The analogy establishes that you wouldn't waive your right to free speech just because you have nothing "important" to say. This highlights the double standard of saying "I don't care about privacy, I have nothing to hide."

The Security Reality

Any backdoor that exists for government agencies or manufacturers is also a backdoor for attackers:

  • Companies and agencies have been hacked before
  • Hackers have stolen data from them before
  • Every time you trust a company with data, you increase chances of leaks
"Boring people are the juiciest targets for identity thieves. Imagine, a great credit score, steady income, and very few bills. Whew! Bring on the mortgages!"

The Flawed Assumption

Arguments against privacy assume that data collectors can be implicitly trusted:

  • Even "boring" data can be used against you (identity theft being just one example)
  • Security breaches happen to all organizations - no company is completely secure
  • Once data is leaked, it's out there forever

Gray Security and Threat Modeling

There's an important paper I reference titled Gray Security that challenges binary security thinking:

  • Viewing security as black/white hinders actual protection
  • Threat modeling is often overlooked in security education
  • This has bred a culture of "X is secure, Y is not" without nuance
  • Even NIST and NSA documents now address this security-usability gap

The Ownership Perspective

Privacy is fundamentally about ownership of information:

  • Even as an honest employee, forced transparency removes your choice to be honest
  • The virtue of honesty is taken from you without compensation
  • Criminals make a choice, law-abiding citizens make another - transparency removes this choice
"It does not matter whether I'm a criminal or not. What matters is that privacy is my property, and if people take it away from me, I try to smack them, because just as you fight for your stuff, I do for mine."

Final Thought

Privacy isn't about hiding - it's about maintaining control over your personal boundaries and information. In an increasingly transparent world, the right to choose what we share becomes ever more precious.
